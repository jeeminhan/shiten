A cultural-difference quiz designed to be taken with an American friend, not alone — so the international student feels seen, and the friend has something specific to say.

APRIL 2026 · 5-MIN READ
01 · The Problem

International students feel friction they can't name. Americans don't know how to meet them.

A Chinese or Indian student shows up on a US campus and, within weeks, is navigating a thousand small cultural mismatches — pressure they can't explain, obligations no one else sees, a quiet loneliness underneath the small talk. Their American friends aren't unkind. They just have no shared vocabulary. "How are you?" / "Good." End of conversation.

The existing Two Lenses quiz (Japanese vs. American cultural instincts) already proves that naming a cultural concept — gaman, amae, senpai-kōhai — gives both people a way in. The student sees their instinct labeled. The friend has a handhold. A real conversation starts.

We want to extend that effect to the two largest international student populations in the US: Chinese and Indian.

02 · How It's Actually Used

Not a quiz you take alone. A quiz you take sitting next to someone.

The use case is specific and shared: the international student pulls up the quiz on their phone while their American friend sits next to them. They read each scenario together. The student picks an answer. The reveal appears — a named cultural concept on the student's side, an American counterpart on the friend's side — and underneath both, a single question aimed at no one in particular: "Tell a time you've been caught between what someone gave you and what you wanted for yourself."

Both read it. Both can answer, or not. The cultures differ on what you do. They converge on what you feel. That convergence is the bridge.

06 · Why This Works

The emotional bridge is the whole idea.

Cultures diverge on what you do. The rules of face-saving, obligation, silence, and deference look radically different across Japan, China, India, and the US. That's what a normal "culture quiz" would stop at — an interesting comparison, mostly forgotten by the next morning.

Cultures converge on what you feel. Fear of standing out, the weight of family, the wish to be known without having to say so, the pressure to endure — these are universal. A Vermont-raised engineering major has felt them too, even if her culture doesn't have a word for them. The bridge question is what surfaces that convergence — and turns a "you're different from me" moment into "oh, I know that feeling."

This reframes the product. It isn't a quiz that teaches one person about another. It's a quiz that gives two people a way to recognize themselves in each other.

07 · Scope

Small surface. Real content. No backend.

The engineering is deliberately modest. The quiz is static, lives on Vercel, stores nothing. The real work is writing culturally resonant content — 10 scenarios × 3 cultures, each with a named concept, a US counterpart, and an emotion-bridge question. That's where the care needs to go, and that's the piece that benefits from cultural-advisor review before ship.

What we'd build

  • Single React app, three quiz routes (/china, /india, /japan)
  • One shared quiz engine, three content files
  • Chinese quiz in Simplified Chinese + English subtitles
  • Indian quiz in English, with native concept names (Hindi/Sanskrit, regional where it fits)
  • Rewrite Japanese quiz's third-perspective section to match the new emotion-bridge pattern
  • Emotional signature panel on the result page
  • Optional light hub at /

What we wouldn't

  • User accounts or saving results
  • Shareable result URLs
  • Analytics or tracking (privacy-by-default for a shared-seating context)
  • Admin UI for editing content (content is code)
  • More cultures in this pass (Korean, Vietnamese, Nigerian, etc. — future)
  • A/B testing on bridge questions
08 · What We Need Your Take On

Open questions for the room.

  1. Is the right MVP all three at once — or ship Chinese first and learn? All three together is a cleaner launch but more content to write well. Chinese-first lets us validate the bridge-question format with real users before investing in the Indian content, which has higher cultural-sensitivity stakes.
  2. Who reviews the content before we ship? We need at least one Chinese and one Indian reader with lived experience to pressure-test every scenario and concept name. Ministry / student-ministry networks? Existing relationships? Paid reviewers?
  3. Where does this live, and whose name is on it? Subdomain of an existing ministry site? Standalone? Open-source so campus groups can fork it for Korean / Vietnamese / Nigerian variants?
  4. Is "quiz" the right frame — or is it something closer to a guided conversation? Right now it's scenario-driven. Is there a version that's more like a shared reading, less like a personality test? Worth exploring before we commit.
  5. What's the ask of each user after they finish? Currently: nothing. No signup, no share-sheet, no "tell your friend." Is that right, or should finishers be invited into something (a community, a follow-up, a contribution loop)?
